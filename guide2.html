U.S. Voter Registration: Frequently Asked Questions (Q&A)

Basic Eligibility

  • Who can register? You must be a U.S. citizen, at least 18 years old by Election Day (some states allow 16–17-year-olds to pre-register), and a resident of the state where you register for at least 30 days.
  • Can felons register? Eligibility varies by state. Some restore voting rights after release; others impose restrictions. Maine, Vermont, and D.C. allow voting during incarceration.
  • Can non-citizens register? No, except a few municipalities allow non-citizen voting in local elections.
  • Can students or homeless individuals register? Yes — students can choose either home or school address; homeless individuals may use a shelter or regular location as their address.

How & Where to Register

  • Online: Most states support online registration via vote.gov.
  • By Mail: Use the National Mail Voter Registration Form (except NH, ND, WI, WY) and mail it with signature and ID/proof if required.
  • In Person: Register at your local election office, DMV, library, or government facility.
  • Same-Day Registration: Allowed in 25 states plus D.C.; North Dakota requires no registration.

Deadlines & Confirming Registration

  • Deadlines vary, typically 2–4 weeks before Election Day; some states allow same-day registration.
  • Check your registration status online at your state election website or vote.gov.
  • You’ll typically receive a voter card within 6–8 weeks after registration.

Identification Requirements

  • Most states require a photo ID (driver’s license, state ID), some accept non-photo IDs like utility bills.
  • If you lack ID, affidavits or provisional ballots may be allowed with later verification.

Special Groups: Military, Overseas, Disabilities

  • Military and overseas voters use the Federal Post Card Application (FPCA) to register and request absentee ballots.
  • Federal law requires accessible registration and polling places for voters with disabilities and elderly voters.

Common Misconceptions & Clarifications

  • Since 1993, the Motor Voter Law requires registration options at DMVs, disability centers, schools, and libraries.
  • Registration is free and does not affect draft status, insurance, or other unrelated issues.

Updating Your Registration

  • If you move, change your name or party, update your registration online, by mail, or in person.
